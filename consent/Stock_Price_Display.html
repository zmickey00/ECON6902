Stock Price Details
Stock Price
This experiment mimics a stock whose value is more likely to rise if it has risen in the last round, and the value is more likely to fall if it has fallen in the last round. It also mimics the noise in the financial market caused by random or irrational behaviors.
More precisely, the stock price is equal to the sum of the value of the stock and the random noise. Your investment will only rise or shrink with the stock price. This experiment tries to mimic the real investment decision-making process so only the stock price will be disclosed to you.

In all games, from the first round to the second round, the value of the stock has the same chance to rise or fall by $0.05. From the second round on, if the value of the stock has increased from last round to this round, then it has a 90% chance to rise by $0.05 and a 10% chance to shrink by $0.05 from this round to next round. Conversely, if the value of the stock has decreased from last round to this round, then it has a 90% chance to shrink by $0.05 and a 10% chance to rise by $0.05 from this round to next round.

The noises are some random dollar amounts. They are independent of each other between rounds and games. In each round, they are randomly drawn from -$0.06 to $0.06 (any number between them can be drawn). Values closer to $0 are more likely to be drawn than those further away. For instance, the noise is more likely to be -$0.015 than $0.056.

The stock price is equal to the sum of the value of the stock and the noise in each round. Hence, be aware that the noise can have a larger magnitude than the change of value. For example, it may be the case that the stock price increased from the last round while the value of the stock decreased from the last round.

The games will only disclose the stock price to you, which can be similar to bottom right graph. As displayed below, the value of the stock in these graphs has been increasing in all rounds (the bottom left graph - you will not see while playing), but there are some rounds (2 to 3, 5 to 6, 7 to 8) that the stock price went down. These graphs are representing the movement of the stock price in a game. When you are playing the game, you will NOT see the future stock price.
Notice that the value of the stock in this example has been increasing in all rounds - this can be the case but NOT necessarily. We will generate the stock price in all the games strictly following the rules above. Again, your investment will only rise or fall with the stock price.

Financial Market
In this experiment, you will play 4 individual games. The games are mostly identical except that they represent different fluctuations of the financial market. To make things simple, these games use the stock price to represent the market's fluctuation. For example, if you have $2 worth of investment and the stock price moves from $1 (this round) to $1.3 (next round), then you will have $2 Ã— $1.3⁄$1 = $2.6 worth of investment in the next round. Your job is to make the most money in each game.
Individual Games
Each game consists of 20 rounds. In each game, you will start with $10 initial endowment. The stock price will be revealed to you in some of the rounds. In the rounds that you can see the stock price, you can choose how much you want to invest into the financial market, so that the amount you invested will grow or shrink with the financial market until the round you can see the stock price again. In those rounds where you do NOT see the stock price, you CANNOT make any investment decision. Moreover, in those rounds that you can see the stock price and make investment decisions, we will pre-withdraw all of your previous investment and add them to your available balance.
